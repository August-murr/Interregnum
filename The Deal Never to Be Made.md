## The Deal Never to Be Made

A deal between the two leading superintelligence projects—one that would benefit America, China, and the rest of the world—will likely never be made due to paranoia, nationalism, and mutual distrust.

### Different Definitions of National Security

The United States tends to view advanced AI through a Cold War lens: the fear that a non-democratic rival could gain overwhelming technological and geopolitical power. The concern is not only what an adversary can do today, but what unknown capabilities they may develop tomorrow.

China's concerns are different. Its priorities center on national security, social harmony, and political stability. It fears AI being used by internal rivals, dissidents, foreign actors, or other groups seeking to undermine the authority of the state and create instability.

In short:

* America is primarily concerned with global influence and strategic competition.
* China is primarily concerned with domestic stability and regime security.

A true superintelligence, by definition, would not belong to any nation. The greater concern is not superintelligence itself, but weaponized and controllable AI developed within systems driven by fear, competition, and political interests.

## Democracy vs. Authoritarianism? Or Nationalism vs. Globalism?

The debate is often framed as democracy versus authoritarianism, but the real divide may be nationalism versus globalism.

A democracy is only trusted when it demonstrates a commitment to its allies and the broader international community. Authoritarian governments are widely distrusted, but it is equally naïve to assume that democratic governments will always act in the interests of the world rather than their own national interests.

The central challenge is not simply the type of government developing AI. It is the growing tendency toward nationalism and isolationism across the world.

As those forces grow stronger, the prospect of globally inclusive AI governance grows weaker.
